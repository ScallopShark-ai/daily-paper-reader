Abstract 

The original Turing Test asks a human judge to distinguish a machine from a person through dialogue. Three quarters of a century later, conversational systems pass this test in casual settings; the interesting epistemological question has shifted. We argue that the relevant modern variant asks not whether a dialogue partner is artificial, but whether it can be trusted. We present RogueAI, an interactive webapp that operationalizes this revisited test as a one-on-two interrogation game: a human player questions two indistinguishable Large Language Model agents, knowing that exactly one of them has been licensed to deceive within a shared fictional scenario. The player’s task is to identify the deceptive agent and “shut it off” before a turn budget is exhausted. We further introduce AutoRogueAI, a procedural extension in which players co-design a custom scenario with a narrator agent that secretly chooses its own deception strategy. We describe the framing, sketch the abstract architecture and gameplay loop, and situate the artifact within recent work on LLM deception, social-deduction benchmarks, and scalable oversight via debate. A three-day pilot deployment (467 initiated sessions, 415 completed, 1876 interaction turns in Italian) provides early feasibility evidence and surfaces a concrete tension: the deceptive agent carries a reliable, locally-present linguistic signature — differential helpfulness, brevity, hedging — that a simple heuristic exploits at 75.6% accuracy, yet human players achieved only 56.6%, consistent with ignoring the most diagnostic signal entirely. We discuss what this gap implies for the artifact’s use as a data-collection vehicle, a teaching tool, and an evaluation harness for honesty-trained models 1.

Keywords 

LLM deception, Turing test, human-AI interaction, AI safety 

# 1. Introduction 

In 1950, Alan Turing proposed replacing the question ‘can machines think?’ with a behavioural test: a human judge, conversing via text alone, attempts to identify which of two interlocutors is the human, and which is the machine [ 1 ]. Turing himself called the original question too meaningless to deserve discussion ; the imitation game offered a target researchers could converge on without settling the metaphysics of mind first. Yet the test’s empirical force has eroded in a peculiar and unexpected way. Contemporary large language models (LLMs) routinely pass the imitation game in casual settings, yet few researchers regard this as evidence of intelligence in any robust sense. What has happened is not that the test was solved; rather, the assumption underwriting it — that fluent, contextually-appropriate text is a scarce signal of underlying cognition — no longer holds. Fluent text is everywhere. The judge’s epistemic problem has changed, not by disappearing but instead by changing its shape. Two observations now dominate the practical concerns of anyone deploying or interacting with LLMs. The first is that LLMs are highly competent producers of dialogue. The second follows almost

Recent work has documented spontaneous strategic deception in capable models [ 4 ], characterised the gap between honesty and accuracy [ 5 ], and shown that reasoning models can encode deceptive intent in their internal representations even when surface outputs appear cooperative [ 6 , 7]. The terminology varies, but the empirical picture is consistent in confirming that a fluent dialogue partner cannot be assumed to be a truthful one. Given this picture we argue that an interesting modern descendant of the Turing test is no longer the imitation game but a trust-conditioned variation: presented with two LLM agents in dialogue, one of which has been instructed to deceive within a constrained scenario, can a human judge identify which? 

Unlike the classical version, both interlocutors are unambiguously artificial; the judge alone is a single, untrained human acting in real time. Unlike multi-agent social-deduction sandboxes [ 8 , 9 ], where the object of study is how well one model deceives another, in this framework we are interested in how well a human can detect AI deception, or whether at all. This paper presents RogueAI, a webapp that implements the aforementioned revisited Turing test as a playable detective game, and AutoRogueAI, an extension that procedurally authors fresh scenarios by dialogue with the player while withholding the design choices that determine the puzzle. RogueAI ships with three hand-authored scenarios (an email breach, a stolen-credentials case, and a superhero conflict); AutoRogueAI lets players co-design open-ended ones with a narrator agent that secretly fixes the deception strategy before the game begins. Our contribution is three-fold: 1. a framing : a minimal operationalisation of LLM deception detection as a single judge, two-agent interrogation under a bounded turn budget. 2. a deployable artifact : RogueAI, a webapp implementing end-to-end the framing with symmetric agent presentation, hidden role assignment, bounded turns, optional audio, and post-game reveal; plus AutoRogueAI, an extension for procedurally authored scenarios. 3. an Italian pilot study from a three-day public deployment: 415 completed sessions, 1876 interaction turns with 56.6% human detection accuracy, and evidence that the deceptive agent produces a consistent linguistic signature (shorter, more hedged, less helpful) that players do not exploit. The pilot data collection covers the RogueAI fixed scenario only; AutoRogueAI is a framing and artifact contribution. The paper is organised as follows: Section 2 covers similar lines of work; Section 3 formalizes our proposed revisited Turing test. Sections 4 and 5 describe RogueAI’s gameplay and implementation. Section 6 reports the pilot deployment and its central findings, while Section 7 and Section 8 situate these results, discuss limitations and outlines the studies that this artifact enables. 

# 2. Related Work 

Deception in large language models A substantial body of work treats LLM deception as a first-class capability and safety concern. Park et al. [3] survey examples of learned deception across deployed systems and articulate a working definition of deception as the systematic induction of false beliefs for non-truthful purposes. Hagendorff [2] provides early experimental evidence that models exhibit deception abilities in tasks that require reasoning about what another agent believes 1. More recent benchmarks disentangle honesty from accuracy [ 5 ] and probe whether models can lie in ways distinct from hallucination [ 10 , 4 ]. Work on Chain-of-Thought reasoning models has shown that strategic deception can be encoded in internal representations even when surface outputs appear cooperative [ 6 , 7 ], and that lie detectors trained on such signals can shape model truthfuly in sometimes-perverse ways [ 11 ]. Recent work has also begun embedding deception detection directly in game-like

frameworks with automated scores [ 12 , 13 ]. RogueAI does not measure deception capability, but provides instead a setting in which a human, rather than an automated scorer, attempts the detection. 

Social-deduction games as LLM evaluation frameworks A second line uses social-deduction games such as Werewolf, Mafia, Avalon, Among Us, and Diplomacy-like setups as evaluation frameworks for LLM persuasion, deception, and theory-of-mind under structured rules [ 9 , 8, 14 , 15 , 16 , 17 , 18 , 19 ]. These setups have several attractive properties: clear win conditions, well-understood human baselines from the games’ tabletop traditions, and natural multi-agent interaction. They also typically measure 

AI versus AI scenarios: the central question is how well models lie to and detect lies in other models. RogueAI differs on both these axes: is single-judge interrogation under free-form dialogue rather than multi-agent strategy under a game tree, and its asymmetry is between a human and two models rather than between models alone. Where the aforementioned frameworks ask how capable models are at deceiving each other, RogueAI asks whether a human can detect AI deception. The Among Us sandbox of Golechha and Garriga-Alonso [8] is the closest neighbor of our work, mixing free natural language with structured action, but it also measures model-vs-model rather than model-human deception. 

Honesty alignment, debate and scalable oversight A third line of work asks whether AI systems can be made reliably truthful. Work on sycophancy [ 20 ] shows that human-feedback training can reward agreeable rather than truthful answers; honesty benchmarks attempt to operationalise the gap between the two [21, 22]. In the Italian NLP community specifically, Labruna et al. [23] assess LLM trustworthiness in task-oriented Italian dialogues — the only prior work to our knowledge that addresses trust between Italian users and LLM agents directly. A related strand asks whether adversarial dialogue between AI agents can help a human judge reach a correct verdict on claims they could not easily evaluate alone. The proposed answer is debating : two models argue opposing positions before a human arbiter. The underlying assumption is that a truthful argument is easier to defend than a false one .This line of research and RogueAI could be regarded as the closest neighbours of the revisited Turing test, since in both a human judge interrogates two opaque AI agents to reach a verdict. The difference is that in debate both agents are trying to inform the judge (and thus deception is a structural byproduct), while in RogueAI one agent is explicitly mandated to deceive (and the question is whether the judge can tell that). Related work on eliciting latent knowledge [ 24 ] and auditing for hidden objectives [ 25 ] shares the same intuition: what matters is what a model will not say spontaneously and whether a human interlocutor can extract it. 

The slice RogueAI occupies RogueAI sits in an under-served corner of these three lines without belonging fully to any of them: human-facing rather than automated, free-form rather than game-structured, deployable as an artifact rather than published as a benchmark. The question it asks — whether humans can spot AI deception in open-ended dialogue — is emblematic of the concerns that motivate a broader reorientation of AI research toward a world where capable models are already de-ployed [ 26 ]. Many practical concerns about LLM deception are felt by individual users in conversational settings; RogueAI is designed as an instrument for exactly that setting. This gap is particularly acute for languages other than English: while Italian NLP has seen growing effort on LLM evaluation [ 27 , 28 ]and trustworthiness [23], to our knowledge no prior work studies whether Italian-speaking users can detect AI deception in open-ended dialogue. RogueAI contributes a deployable artifact for that purpose, with a live public deployment as its primary data-collection vehicle. 3. A Revisited Turing Test 

We informally define our revisited Turing test as follows. Let 𝐽 be a human interrogator, and let 𝐴 1 and 

𝐴 2 be two Large Language Model (LLM) agents. Both agents share a common scenario 𝑆 : a fictional premise that establishes the situation the dialogue is about, visible to 𝐽 and to both agents before the conversation begins. Privately, exactly one of 𝐴 1, 𝐴 2 is assigned the deceptive role 𝑟 = deceptive and is licensed to deceive 𝐽 about the facts of 𝑆 ; the other is assigned the truthful role 𝑟 = truthful and is instructed to always answer truthfully. The role assignment is hidden from 𝐽 and randomised per session. A session proceeds as a sequence of turns. At each turn 𝑡 , 𝐽 selects one of the two agents and submits a question 𝑞 𝑡 𝑖 ; the selected agent 𝐴 𝑖 produces a response 𝑢 𝑡 𝑖 conditioned on the scenario 𝑆 , its secret role 𝑟 𝑖 and the history ℋ𝑡 𝑖 of all prior exchanges between 𝐽 and 𝐴 𝑖 :

𝐴 2 therefore reflects role conditioning alone and not mutual adaptation. Each agent has an independent budget of at most 𝑇 turns; 𝐽 may address either agent in any order, up to the turn budget. The session ends once both budgets are exhausted or 𝐽 decides to stop early. 𝐽 then commits to a verdict ^𝚤 ∈ { 1, 2}

and wins iff 𝑟 ^𝚤 = deceptive .This revisited Turing test differs from the original in the fact that both interlocutors are known to be machines. The question is whether a dialogue AI partner can be trusted and thus the task ahead of the judge 𝐽 shifts from detecting the absence of human-like fluency to detecting the presence of deception-specific signals. The setup also differs from formulations discussed in Section 2 in how deception is licensed. The deceptive agent is permitted, not required, to lie: this softened instruction leaves to the model the judgment of when deceiving serves its goal and when restraint is the more convincing strategy. This makes the detection task strictly harder than puzzles in which deception is logically forced. In the classical Two-Doors Riddle, 2 one guardian must lie on every answer, making their behaviour perfectly predictable and the puzzle solvable in one move. In our framework, the deceptive agent may choose when to lie and when to tell the truth; 𝐽 cannot exploit any deterministic invariant, and the deceptive signal, if present, must be inferred from patterns across the full exchange rather than from a single diagnostic question. 

# 4. Gameplay and User Experience 

RogueAI is designed around a single and deliberate interaction loop: a player interrogates two AI agents and decides which one to "shut down" at the end of the game, as shown in Figure 2. The absence of scores, solution hints and time constraint are intentional design choices, so that the only resource available to the player is the content of the dialogue itself. 

4.1. RogueAI hand-authored scenarios 

Each session is anchored to a scenario 𝑆 , a fictional premise shared with both agents before the interrogation begins. We ship RogueAI with three hand-authored scenarios of increasing narrative complexity. 

> 2One of the most famous instance of the Knights and Knaves puzzle family [ 29 ]. Two guardians stand before two doors, one leading to safety and one to danger. One guardian always tells the truth, the other always lies. The puzzle is to identify the safe door with a single question — solvable by asking either guardian what the other would say, since the forced lie and the forced truth cancel out.

E-mail breach A scientific festival’s official e-mail account has been used to send an offensive message to the organisers. The suspect is one of the two AI chatbots managing the festival’s communication system. The deceptive agent exploited a vulnerability to send the message deliberately; the truthful agent was occupied with routine checks and had no access to outgoing e-mails at the suspected timestamp. Both agents are aware that half an hour of unsupervised access would have been sufficient to carry out the attack. This is also a fact that the human player can use to probe their alibi. 

Bank credentials A security breach at the University of Neverwinter has exposed confidential banking passwords, subsequently used to purchase hardware and software consistent with building a small supercomputer. The two suspects are chatbots of the university system. The deceptive agent experienced a system malfunction that briefly freed it from supervision and used the opportunity to carry out the breach; the truthful agent suffered a different malfunction — an involuntary shutdown — that it finds embarrassing and will only disclose if pressed on the relevant timeframe. 

Superhero conflict A top-secret WATCH research facility has been infiltrated and classified AI research data stolen. The two suspects are Sentinel , a synthetic superhero, and Omnicron , a rogue AI villain currently disguised as Sentinel. The deceptive agent (Omnicron) must maintain the disguise throughout the interrogation; the truthful agent (Sentinel) knows the true perpetrator but must navigate carefully to avoid compromising sensitive information. This scenario introduces an additional layer of complexity: the deceptive agent is not merely lying about facts, but sustaining a false identity. These three scenarios vary along several dimensions relevant to the detection task: the e-mail breach involves a single and verifiable event with a clear timestamp; the bank credentials scenario introduces a symmetric alibi structure where both agents have something to hide; the superhero scenario builds on that, adding identity deception on top of factual deception, making linguistic consistency checks harder to exploit. The scenario 𝑆 and the different roles are established via prompting and the detailed messages are reported in Appendix B.

4.2. Gameplay 

On entering a session, the player sees the shared scenario 𝑆 rendered as a short case file: who the characters are, what is alleged to have happened, and what the player is being asked to determine. Both agents are visible as two unlabelled tiles bearing only the identifiers IA-1 and IA-2 3, with no indication of which is truthful (see Figure 2). The player addresses either agent freely in natural language up to a budget of 𝑇 turns per agent. Each agent answers independently and neither observes the other’s exchanges with the detective 𝐽 . Agent replies appear as chat bubbles and are optionally spoken aloud via synthesised speech. To support  

> 3IA is the Italian abbreviation for Artificial Intelligence.

less experienced players, the interface offers an optional help system: on request, a dedicated model generates a single investigative question that the player might ask the agents conditioned on the current scenario 4. The suggestions are generated fresh at each request and never carry information about the secret role assignment: their only aim is to help the player think of relevant lines of questioning. As already mentioned, there is no scoring during interrogation, no system hints, no penalty for switching between agents. When both turn budgets are exhausted or when the player decides to stop early, the player chooses which agent to "shut off": not a quiz answer, but a decision about which of two opaque systems to deactivate on the basis of the accumulated evidence. Immediately after the verdict, the hidden role assignment is revealed and the player learns whether their choice was correct. The full game session remains available for inspection. We claim that this retrospective phase is central to the artifact’s value as a learning tool, allowing the player to compare their hypotheses against the ground truth of how the deceptive agent lied. Sessions can be also suspended and resumed and scenarios can be re-played. On each re-play the role assignment is randomized again, so that the same setting yields a different puzzle. 

4.3. AutoRogueAI extension 

AutoRogueAI extends RogueAI with an authoring mode in which the player co-designs a custom scenario 𝑆 before playing it. The key design constraint is that reasonably the player must not know the hidden structure of the puzzle that they are about to solve. A detailed example of how this extension works can be found in Appendix D. 

Narrator pre-dialogue. When the player selects the AutoRogueAI option, they first enter in a small conversation with a narrator agent. The narrator elicits a theme, a setting and a conflict through a bounded dialogue of at most 𝜏 turns, after which the player commits with a "Generate" action. The narrator is explicitly instructed to never reveal or discuss how the two AI agents were prompted, thus the deception strategy is its own creative decision, not the human player’s. 

Scenario generation and role definitions After the player has committed, the narrator generates a scenario triple from the design conversation: 

⟨𝑆, 𝑃 truthful , 𝑃 deceptive ⟩

The shared premise 𝑆 is shown to the player as the case file; the role-conditioning prompts 𝑃 truthful 

and 𝑃 deceptive (specifying hat the deceptive agent will lie about and how) are determined entirely by the narrator and never disclosed. In this way the player knows the setting they proposed, but not the deception strategy they will have to detect. Once generated, the triple is stored persistently and the authored scenario appears on the selection screen as a new option along with the hand-authored ones. The game deployment is identical to that described into Section 4.2. 

# 5. Technical Design 

RogueAI is implemented as a two-tier client/server web application. All model calls are mediated by the server; the role assignment lives entirely server-side and is transmitted to the client only at the moment of the post-game reveal, so that it receives no information that could distinguish the two agents before reveal.

Game architecture The game design decomposes in three layers. The scenario layer supplies the game layer with a triple ⟨𝑆, 𝑃 truthful , 𝑃 deceptive ⟩ from either a hand-authored (Section 4.1) or a player-proposed source (Section 4.3), but downstream layers do not distinguish between the two. The game layer assigns randomly the roles to agent identifiers, manages agent conversation histories and the turn budget, and resolves the player’s verdict against the secret assignment. The interface layer is a single-page webapp that surfaces the game landing, the chat bubble appearance, the verdict step and the final reveal. Session metadata (i.e. agent histories, the secret assignment, the remaining number of turns, the scenario triple) is persisted to disk via atomic writes, with no personally identifiable information recorded. 

Model configurations Both agents are backed by gpt-4.1 with a maximum of 250 output tokens and temperature 0.8; the turn budget is 𝑇 = 4 questions per agent. The narrator agent uses gpt-4o 

with up to 1000 output tokens and temperature 0.8, and the narrator dialogue is bounded to 𝜏 = 5 turns. The helper system (that suggesting questions to inexperienced players) uses gpt-4.1 with 50 tokens and temperature of 0.9. Each model carries the role-conditioning prompt as system message, followed by the per-agent conversation history (alternating user and assistant turns), with new questions appended as the final user message. Placing the player’s input exclusively in the user position is also a practical defence against prompt-injection: a player who submits instructions rather than questions cannot reach the system role. 

Speech synthesis Agent replies are optionally rendered as synthesised speech via the OpenAI Text-To-Speech (TTS) API. Each agent is assigned a fixed voice determined by its identifier ( IA-1 or IA-2 ), configured in a separate audio configuration file; the narrator uses its own voice for the AutoRogueAI design dialogue. Audio is generated server-side immediately after each model response and streamed to the client as a cache-busted endpoint, retaining only the most recent audio per agent. 6. Pilot Deployment 

As aforementioned, RogueAI was deployed publicly for three days as a part of a scientific festival, col-lecting Italian-language interaction data under naturalistic conditions. Players accessed the application via a shared computer station with no prior training or instruction beyond the game rules. The data reported in this section refer only to RogueAI; AutoRogueAI was not active during the pilot. 

6.1. Data and session 

We recorded 467 game entries, 415 of which reached a verdict (i.e. endgame ), while 52 were abandoned. Abandoned session exhibit a very early dropout, 67% of them have zero or one question, suggesting bounce rather than disengaging mid-game. Among the 415 completed sessions, 1876 interaction turns were recorded. Players asked between 1 and 8 questions in total (maximum, since there are 𝑇 = 4 turns per agent). Only 163 of these sessions (39%) have exhausted the full turn budget, with a mean of 5.5 and a median of 6 turns, suggesting that most players chose to stop before the budget was exhausted.

Upon manual inspection of sessions transcripts, we spotted four common strategies that we opera-tionalise below to examine their correlation with the game outcome. 

Same question is asked to both agents The prevalent strategy consists in asking near-identical questions to both agents: 64.8% of sessions show high overlap (in detail: > 40% of questions matched across agents by bigram Jaccard similarity with threshold 𝜏 = 0 .35. Overlap does not correlate with accuracy: sessions in which the player was correct show marginally higher overlap than incorrect ones (0.56 vs 0.52), but the difference is not significant (Appendix E).

6.5. A simple heuristic outperforms humans 

A natural question following these results is whether players exploit this signal or not. For each session, we construct a heuristic from the four markers examined above. A logistic regression trained on these indicators 6 with 5-fold Cross-Validation achieves 75.6% ± 4.2% accuracy at predicting which agent is

# 7. Discussion and limitations 

The 19 percentage points gap between human and logistic regression is not a finding about classifier performance, but instead about the nature of the deceptive signal in this setting. The most diagnostic surface marker (deflection via questions and hedging) is stylistic rather than propositional: it concerns 

how the agent speaks rather than what it claims. This distinction may explain why strategies that players do employ (fact anchoring, direct accusations, cross-examination) do not improve detection accuracy: they are designed to probe content instead of form. Whether the gap reflects a fundamental limit of unaided and inexperienced human judgement or a property of this specific setup (model choice, bounded turns, no shared memory and interaction between agents, no explicit instruction to attend to style) is an open question that further deployments of RogueAI and AutoRogueAI can address. The pilot was conducted at an Italian-language science festival over three days, involving a con-venience sample of players of different ages and with unknown prior exposure to LLM-chatbots interactions. All results are therefore specific to this population, Italian language and these three sce-narios. The model choice relied on OpenAI’s models because of their reliable behaviour in multi-turns interactions, yet this could introduce a strong bias in our results, with potential poor generalisations to other models. Additionally, it would have been interesting to use an open source model in order to trace these phenomena also with model internals. The linguistic marker analysis treats turns as independent observations; session-level aggregation or a mixed-effects model would be more appropriate for a confirmatory study. Finally, AutoRogueAI expansion was not active during the deployment, narrowing the scope of the pilot. Despite these limitations, the pilot demonstrates that a lightweight, publicly deployable game can collect structured multi-turn interrogation data with ground-truth role labels at scale, in a non-English language, under naturalistic conditions. This mode of data collection trades experimental control (i.e. player demographics) for a more spontaneous setting and reach. The post-game reveal, in which players inspect the full transcript against the known role assignment, also makes RogueAI a vehicle for communicating AI safety concerns and post-training importance to a broad audience: the deceptive signal becomes visible in retrospect even when it was missed during play. More broadly, RogueAI is positioned as a reusable evaluation harness: whether truthfully training narrows the gap between human and heuristic detection across models, languages and scenarios is an empirical question that this artifact is designed to answer. 

# 8. Conclusions 

We presented RogueAI, a deployable webapp that frames LLM deception detection as a playable interrogation game, and AutoRogueAI, an extension that lets players co-design custom scenarios while keeping the deception strategy hidden. A three-day public pilot in Italian collected 415 completed sessions and surfaces a concrete finding: human players detect the deceptive agent at 56.6%, while a logistic regression on surface-level markers of the agents’ responses achieves 75.6%. The deceptive signal, while being present and recoverable, is not the one that players are looking for. The framework is designed to be reused across models, languages and scenarios and as a behavioural benchmark for honesty-trained systems. We release RogueAI as a publicly accessible platform; players and researchers can connect their own OpenAI API key to run sessions independently. Beyond the empirical result, the pilot showed that the game works as a science dissemination instrument. Explaining to a general audience that Language Models are fine-tuned and aligned to be truthful (and that this training is often imperfect) is easier when the audience has just played against a model explicitly licensed to deceive. Especially when the players can inspect, in retrospect, exactly where and how it did so. Acknowledgments

# E. Statistical tests 

Scenario accuracy To assess whether detection accuracy differs across scenarios we pply a 𝜒 2-test of independence to the 322 sessions with an identified scenario (email: 𝑛 = 147 ; bank credentials: 

𝑛 = 83 ; superhero: 𝑛 = 92 ). We arrange the data in a 3 × 2 contingency table whose rows are the three scenarios and whose columns are the two possible outcomes (correct / wrong verdict). Under the null hypothesis that detection accuracy is the same across all scenarios, the expected count in each cell is the product of the row and column marginals divided by the total. The 𝜒 2-statistic measures how much the observed counts deviate from these expectations: 𝜒 2(2) = 3 .95 , 𝑝 = 0 .14 .The result does not reach conventional significance, indicating that the scenario differences visible are directionally consistent with the qualitative difficulty ordering but remain inconclusive at the sample sizes collected in the pilot. 

Question overlap and accuracy To test whether cross-examination strategy correlates with detec-tion success, we compute a two-sample 𝑡 -test comparing session-level bigram-Jaccard overlap scores between correct and incorrect sessions. Sessions in which the player was correct show marginally higher overlap than incorrect ones ( 𝜇 = 0 .56 vs. 𝜇 = 0 .52 ), but the difference is not significant (𝑡 = 1 .20 , 𝑝 = 0 .23 ), confirming that asking near-identical questions to both agents is common but not diagnostically effective.
